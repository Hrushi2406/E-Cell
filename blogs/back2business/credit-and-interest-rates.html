Credit & Interest rates - The life blood of an economy
by Nihar Joshi



The concept of economy is very hard to fathom not just for ordinary people but also for policy makers, leading to unnecessary economic suffering. In simple terms, the economy is made up of sum of transactions that occur when goods and services are bought or sold. For instance, in a transaction there is a buyer who gives money or credit to the seller in exchange for goods, services or financial assets. In the economy, the credit works just like money because either one can be spent the same way in a transaction hence the total spending of money & credit makes up the total economy because it translates into the worth of goods/services bought or sold (also known as the GDP). The economic growth, which has been a topic of heated debate since the pandemic began, occurs in cycles of boom and busts & it is majorly dictated by 2 things- Credit and Interest rates.


Credit can be difficult to understand because it can be created out of thin air! Credit or debt is created when the lender trusts the borrower and lends the money which has to be paid back with interest. So credit essentially allows for spending of money that we don’t really have, resulting in much higher spending and in turn growing incomes because remember someone’s spending is other person’s income! Easily available credit is accompanied by growing income which allows for even greater borrowing as banks are more willing to lend because they have more trust in the borrowers. This forms that part of the cycle which is inflationary; where economic growth occurs. To facilitate this easy credit, interest rates are kept low by the central bank. However, high growth leads to inflation where prices of everyday goods rise dramatically, leading to social and political instability. So to reduce this inflation, the central bank decides to increase the interest rates. Now easy credit vanishes, taking out loans is very costly and borrowers get squeezed. The existing debt becomes more expensive and the economy enters a deflationary phase in its cycle where inflation rapidly reduces and economic growth is moot or even negative.


At the start of 2020, the pandemic created an unprecedented economic crisis; a global shutdown unimaginable in scale. Due to this crisis, the central banks around the world lowered the interest rates dramatically to soften the blow from a massive economic contraction, starting with the USA central bank (The FED) bringing down interest rates to zero! The money printer was switched on in March and it hasn’t stopped since. The economies were flooded with additional liquidity not only because of more money being printed but also very very easy credit. Naturally, this excess and found its way into different asset classes like the stocks & bond. Despite the best efforts, there was a record economic contraction in the most of the global economies, though future prospects continue look to be better. It begs the question of what will happen when the central banks raise the interest rates and the economy enters a deflationary phase? How will pay the huge load of debt and how? These questions haunt the leading world economists today & only time will tell if we can get out of this already ugly recession without further long-term damage.
